Fast Fourier Transform

Discrete Fourier Transform can be regarded as evaluating a polynomial of degree N-\/1 on the powers $ \omega^0, \omega, \omega^2, ..., \omega^(N-1) $ where $ \omega $ is the Nth root of unity.

Given a polynomial of even degree \[ p(t) = a_0 + a_1 t + a_2 t^2 + ... \] we find\+: \[ p(t) = a_0 + a_2 t^2 + a_4 t^4 + ... + t(a_1 + a_3 t^2 + ...) = q_{even}(t^2) + t q_{odd} (t^2) \] where q\+\_\+e and q\+\_\+o are polynomials formed with the even and odd coefficients of p(t). Thus, we get

p(1) = q\+\_\+\{even\}(1) + \textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$0 q\+\_\+\{odd\}(1) p(\textbackslash{}omega) = q\+\_\+\{even\}(\textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$2) + \textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$1 q\+\_\+\{odd\}(\textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$2) p(\textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$2) = q\+\_\+\{even\}(\textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$4) + \textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$2 q\+\_\+\{odd\}(\textbackslash{}omega$^\wedge$4) ...

Note how on the RHS the Fourier transforms of q\+\_\+e and q\+\_\+o appear. Thus\+: Discrete Fourier transform type.
